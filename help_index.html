Library Catalog User Guide

The Library Catalog is the primary gateway to information resources in all formats (including electronic) that are part of the collections at the University of Texas at Austin. The Catalog is a public database that anyone can use at any time. For further information about the University Libraries (services, hours, locations, access policies, and much more), visit the Libraries' web site or click the "Home" link at the top of every screen.

If you have questions about using the Library Catalog, go to the Ask a Librarian site to send a message or chat live with a librarian.

SEARCH OPTIONS

Keyword (Basic)

This is the default search for the Library Catalog. Type one or more keywords in the box. These could include topical concepts, personal author/performer/composer names, organization or conference names, words from subject headings, or any other words that might appear in a catalog record. Keyword searches access only the words found in bibliographic records in the Library Catalog. You are not searching the full text of a book or articles within periodicals.

The more words you type, the more focused your search becomes. If you retrieve no or too few hits, try re-entering your search with fewer terms. If you retrieve too many hits, try re-entering your search with additional terms. All search terms are case-insensitive.

The order of the words entered does not matter. If you type two or more words, the Catalog assumes an AND operator, which means that all words you type must be found in a record. To search an exact phrase, enclose it in quotation marks.

Keyword (Advanced Search)

Advanced Keyword Search offers the most flexible set of options for locating information in the Library Catalog.

The search form has a text box where you may type one or more words and combine them with Boolean operators (AND, OR, AND NOT) to indicate how the terms must be related to each other. Below the search box are optional limits you may place on your search results.

Boolean Operators available include:

  • AND - Both words must occur somewhere in the record (implied by default)
  • OR - One or both words must occur somewhere in the record.
  • AND NOT - Excludes any record containing the following word.
  • " ... " - Quotation marks are used to denote exact phrases: the words must occur in the order entered.

You can also use prefixes in front of words to specify which fields to search. Do not leave a space between the colon and the following character.

  • Title (t:)
  • Author (a:)
  • Subject Heading (d:) (d stands for Descriptor)
  • Note (n:). (Note fields contain many kinds of additional information about the item, including contents, formats, origins, languages, details about such film casts, listing of songs or musical pieces on compact discs, etc. Some records have extensive notes, while others have few. Most note fields are keyword-searchable.)

You can construct a complex search query using Boolean operators, field prefixes, wildcards, and parentheses. (All words and operators are case-insensitive but operators are shown in caps below for clarity.) Examples:

(car* or automobile*) AND (exhaust or emission*)
A form of the word "car" or the word "automobile" must be accompanied by either the word "exhaust" or a form of "emission" anywhere in the record. Typing the AND is optional; it's implied.
"climate change" OR "global warming"
The phrase "climate change" or the phrase "global warming" (exactly as typed) must occur somewhere in the record.
a:mozart t:(sonatas OR fugues)
Mozart as an author with either "sonatas" or "fugues" in a title. The AND linking the a: term and the t: terms is implied, but you can include it if you wish.
a:brown a:foote
Both are author names to be searched separately in author fields. The AND is implied. Note: The search a:(brown foote) will not work here because the two author names are in different author fields.
a:(richard feynman)
The words must occur within a single author field, i.e. parts of the same author's name. With the AND operator (typed or implied) the order of the words does not matter. This kind of search can also be applied to conference names and corporate author names: a:(solar energy) or a:(electrochemical society)
a:smith texas
Author name "smith" with the word "texas" anywhere in the record. The AND is implied.
a:woodward AND d:(watergate OR nixon)
Author name "woodward" with subject headings containing either "watergate" or "nixon".
t:texas AND n:yarborough
The word "texas" in a title with the name "yarborough" in a note field.
d:(world war) clemenceau
Words "world war" in a subject heading and the name "clemenceau" anywhere in the record. The AND operators are implied.
web AND NOT d:spider*
Finds "web" anywhere in the record but excludes records with forms of the word "spider" in a subject heading.
"cattle ranch"~3
"cattle" must appear in the same field as words with "ranch" with no more than three word in between.

You may apply one or more Limit options to your search. The Limits available include:

  • Available items (items not checked out)
  • Items in a specific library location or branch
  • Specific material types and formats (1)
  • Specific languages (1)
  • Publication year or range of years (2)
  • Publisher

1. You can select multiple entries from this menu by holding down the Control key (Windows) or Apple key (Macintosh) while clicking on desired entries.

2. Dates are exclusive: If you enter After 2003 Before 2005 you will retrieve only records with dates of 2004.

You can select a sorting parameter (Date or Title) in place of the default Relevancy Ranking categories. Note: If you use a field limiter in your query, or if you use quotation marks to designate a phrase, the ranking of results is by date, and can't be changed.

See the Limiting section of the Guide for more information on limiting keyword searches.

KNOWN ISSUE: Modify Search Button from Keyword Search Results
When you click the Modify Search button at the top of a keyword search results screen, you will return to the Advanced Search page, and your keyword query will be retained. However, if you selected choices from the Locations, Material Types, or Language menus, these selections are not retained. This is a result of the way those menus are programmed, and can't be fixed.

See the Search Tips section for more information on Keyword searching techniques.

Title

This is a browse search. If you know the first words of a title, use this search. If you have less information, use the Keyword Search to locate a title with a certain word or word combination. A title search will retrieve alternate titles and series titles in addition to regular titles.

Type the title in exact order. Right-hand truncation is implied, so you can stop typing as soon as you have entered enough of the title to be distinctive.

You should omit initial articles: A, AN, and THE in English (although the system will automatically ignore these anyway), and the equivalent articles in other languages. If you want to force a search on an initial article just enclose it in quotes ("a", "an", "the").

All searches are case-insensitive and you can omit punctuation and diacritics.

Examples:

To find: THE OLD MAN AND THE SEA, type: old man and the sea

To find: L'ÉTRANGER, type: etranger

To find: DIE FLEDERMAUS, type fledermaus

To find: JOURNAL OF APPLIED MECHANICS, type: journal of applied mech

To find: SLAUGHTERHOUSE FIVE, type: slaughterhouse f

KNOWN ISSUE: "Hidden" entries in Title browse results
Occasionally when you do a Title browse search, the title you're looking for will not be evident in the list of results. Instead, it will be "hidden" inside a group of records under a totally unrelated title that appears first in the list. It affects records that contain multiple titles and you'll see it most often related to music albums, story anthologies, and other collections that have titles within titles. This is not actually a bug, since the system is designed to behave this way, but it's confusing. This affects only Title browse searches, and you will not encounter it in author, keyword, author+title, or other searches. Another workaround is to select "Title" in the dropdown sorting menu and then click "Sort". This forces the display into brief record mode and makes the list less confusing.

Journal Title

Two separate search options are available on this page. (Why is this?) You should use both options in turn to ensure complete coverage of availability.

Remember that these searches find journals and other serials in library collections, NOT their contents. If you're looking for articles, you'll need to use a database that indexes articles. Go to the How Do I Find Articles page for more information.

Serial Title

The top search box is a title browse search that finds only serials that are included in the Library Catalog: periodicals, journals, magazines, newspapers, annuals, and other serially published works. If you know you're looking for a serial (and not a book or other single work), this is a more precise alternative to a general Title search. If you don't know the exact title of the journal or periodical, use the Keyword search options instead. If you do not find the journal you're looking for, use the E-Journal search option as well.

Omit initial articles (A, AN, THE and non-English equivalents) from your query, and type as much of the title as you know in exact word order. Right-hand truncation is implied.

To find: JOURNAL OF MECHANICAL DESIGN, type:

journal of mechanical design

OR

journal of mechanical des

Don't use abbreviated journal titles such as:

J. Mech. Des.

E-Journal Title

This search queries the Libraries' electronic journal database, which is separate from the Library Catalog. You can enter an exact title or words from a title to retrieve entries for periodicals and journals for which we have some form of electronic access. To return to the Library Catalog after this search, click your browser's Back button.

The E-Journal search does not retrieve information about printed journals and periodicals that might be held in the library collections.

Why are there TWO search boxes?

Unfortunately, information about journal availability is contained in two separate databases, which must be searched independently. The Libraries are exploring ways to integrate this information better, but for the time being you have to search in both places to be sure you're covering all the bases. A quick summary of the two databases:

  1. Library Catalog: contains all serials with print or microform holdings in UT Libraries; serials with some print holdings AND electronic access; some but not all electronic-only serials.

  2. E-Journals Database (powered by SFX): contains all serials with some electronic access, including many not represented in Library Catalog.

If you search both databases and do not find what you're looking for, you can submit a request for an article via Interlibrary Services (link at top of every catalog page), or ask for help at a reference desk.

Author

This is a browse search. Authors may be persons (including co-authors, editors, compilers, translators, composers, performers), organizations, musical groups, government agencies, or conference names.

If you know the author's full name (or most of it), use this search. If you have less information, use the Keyword Search options (Basic or Advanced). Using Advanced Search allows you to combine an author name with a keyword from the title or other field. The Author + Title search option combines an author's name with word(s) from the title.

To search for a person, type the name in reverse order (last name, first name, middle name or initial if known). You don't need to use any punctuation in your query. Search terms are not case-sensitive.

For example,

to find: ARTHUR C. CLARKE type: clarke arthur c or clarke, arthur c

Note: Birth and/or death dates sometimes displayed with an author's name are not part of the index entry and should not be included in your query.

To search for an organization, type enough of the name to be distinctive.

For example,

to find: AMERICAN ACADEMY OF POLITICAL AND SOCIAL SCIENCE

type: american academy of pol

If your first search does not produce the expected results, try searching with different forms:

  • INITIALS........ try SAMUELSON P as well as SAMUELSON PAUL
  • ABBREVIATIONS... try CORP as well as CORPORATION
  • NUMBERS......... try 19TH as well as NINETEENTH
  • VARIANT SPELLINGS... try CO-OPERATION as well as COOPERATION

Author + Title

The Author + Title search option allows you to enter the name of an author (last name first) and combine it with a word from a title. This option is useful for authors or composers with large numbers of publications and editions.

Example:

To find editions of THE TAMING OF THE SHREW by William Shakespeare, type shakespeare william in the Author box, and shrew in the Title box.

You can limit this search to a specific library location at this point in your search if you wish. After you get results, you can click the Limit/Sort button to narrow your results by year, format, or language.

Because the Author + Title search is actually two searches, it is a bit slower than other search types. You can speed it up a bit by entering an author's last and first names instead of just the last name.

Subject Heading

Use the Subject Heading Search to browse the alphabetical Subject Index, which contains a controlled vocabulary of subjects. Subject headings often include detailed subheadings that subdivide a heading by topical, geographical, chronological, and format aspects. The Library Catalog uses Library of Congress Subject Headings (LCSH) and MeSH (medical subject) headings.

The Subject Index includes the following types of entries:

  • Topic or concept........... FOREIGN TRADE REGULATION
  • Person as subject.......... KING MARTIN LUTHER
  • Organization as subject.... HARVARD UNIVERSITY
  • Literary work as subject... HAMLET

or... SHAKESPEARE WILLIAM 1564-1616 HAMLET

If you are not familiar with the structure of Subject Heading entries and their subdivisions, you can start with a Keyword search instead. To identify a valid subject heading to search, start with a Keyword search, then:

  1. click the title to see the Full Display of a specific item
  2. click on one of the SUBJECTS links to search all records for that Subject Heading.

Call Number

Use the Call Number Search form to find a specific item, or to browse the shelves virtually within a subject area.

  • Leave a space between each segment of the call number.
  • Include any punctuation, usually a decimal point.
  • You may type letters in lowercase or uppercase.
  • Do not confuse letters and numbers (examples: O for 0, or I for 1).
  • Do not type oversize codes (-F- or -Q-) or the library name.
  • Records from the Law Library's catalog are not searchable by call number; use Tallons instead.
  • Electronic-only materials in the catalog (such as e-books and e-only journals) do not have call numbers.

When you do a call number search for something with multiple pieces attached to the same parent record, such as a journal with many volumes, the results display one copy of that record for each item attached -- possibly hundreds -- and each display shows the same top three items. To see a list of all attached items, click on the title, then click the View All Copies/Volumes button. Or you can do the search by title or keyword to avoid this multiple display.

The Call Number Search form has four different choices depending on the kind of call number system used:

Library of Congress Call Number

Most books and periodicals cataloged after 1970 are classified and shelved according to the Library of Congress call number system. Call numbers are based on the item's subject. LC call numbers consist of a combination of letters and numbers. The first two segments represent a subject area; the number is filed as a whole number. The following segment(s) combine a letter with a decimal number. A date may be appended at the end, followed by non-searchable volume-specific information.

Examples:
PS 3515 E288 S773 1981
tk 7874.55 m634

Other Call Number

These call number types are used for non-book formats such as media, maps, microforms, dissertations and theses, and non-federal government documents. Others are used for various special collections in campus libraries. Call numbers are case-insensitive and can be typed in uppercase or lowercase. Many older materials (generally pre-1970) in our collections have Dewey Decimal call numbers, which start in most cases with three digits or three digits followed by a decimal and more numbers, followed by a space and additional elements combining letters and numbers. Type letters in either uppercase or lowercase; leave spaces between the elements of the call number.

Examples:

Examples:

U.S. Document Number

The numbers accessed by this search are classification numbers assigned to United States federal government documents by the U.S. Superintendent of Documents in Washington, D.C. These numbers are based on the originating government body or agency.

Type the number including all punctuation. You may include or omit spaces; the search will be processed omitting any spaces in the number. Searches are case-insensitive. You can type only the first part of the number if you wish to see other documents nearby.

Examples:
y4.f76/1:c43/5
ep4.2/ac4/2
i49.2

Most federal documents held in the University Libraries use the U.S. Superintendent of Documents (SuDocs) classification number as a call number. However, some U.S. documents are assigned a different call number in the Catalog, and others are available only on the Web. The document number search option will also retrieve these.

Some U.S. documents, especially those published before 1980, are not listed in the Library Catalog. If you cannot find a specific document, ask for help at the PCL Information and Research Help Desk or send a question to Ask a Librarian.

Call Number As Subject Search Alternative

When you know the Library of Congress or Dewey call number of a relevant item, you may find more items on the same subject by typing the first one or two call number elements. Typing fewer elements widens the subject range of call numbers retrieved.

For example, the call number QD 79 C4 (for Chromatography) could be shortened to QD 79 to locate other items about analytical chemistry in general.

Call numbers are not consistently assigned over time, and materials on the same subject may be scattered across several different call number areas and classification systems. Browsing by call number is thus not a reliable substitute for doing other types of topical searches.

Other call numbers are not based on subject, so browsing these is not meaningful. Government Document call numbers are based on provenance (the originating government agency or subdivision), not subject.

ISBN or Other Number

You can search for materials by standard numbers that are included in the catalog record. If your search gets a single exact match, the full record is displayed. If there is not an exact match, you will see a list of entries starting at the point in the number index where your query would have appeared. This is a browse search.

International Standard Book Number (ISBN)

The ISBN is a 10- or 13-digit number with or without hyphens. Examples:

3-527-27397-2; 978-0-4020-6119-6. Type the number with or without punctuation.

International Standard Serial Number (ISSN)

The ISSN is an 8-digit number with a hyphen in the middle. Example:; 0034-4567. Type the number with or without punctuation.

The ISSN is assigned to journals, periodicals, and publications in series, so this search may retrieve multiple records. Serials with both print and electronic versions have separate ISSNs for each format.

Music Publisher Number

A music publisher number can be entered with or without spaces. Examples: ETC 1022; DE3155

Library of Congress Control Number (LCCN)

This number is an accession number from the Library of Congress’ catalog. It often appears on the back of the title page of older books and predates the ISBN. The first segment is a 2- or 4-digit year, followed by a six digit number. If you have an LCCN that contains fewer digits (ex. 86-1782), you must add zeros in front of the second segment to total six digits (86-001782). Maps have the prefix "MAP". Typing the hyphen is optional. Examples: 57-000012; 2003000331; map59000003

CODEN

CODENs are six-character codes assigned to serials by the Chemical Abstracts Service. They have largely been supplanted by the ISSN. Examples: JBBID4; malaaj

OCLC NUMBER

The OCLC number is a unique control number from OCLC (WorldCat), a bibliographic database representing the holdings of thousands of libraries throughout the world. It is helpful to include the OCLC number when submitting an Interlibrary Services request.

SEARCH TIPS

Limiting (Keyword Search)

You may apply one or more Limit options to your search via the Advanced Search form. The Limits available include:

Available items If you want to limit your results to items that are available in the library, check this box. You will retrieve only records that have at least one item that is available for check-out or use in the library. (Other attached items that are not available because they are currently checked out or otherwise unavailable may show up on the same records.)

Items in a specific library location or branch: Select one location from the pull-down menu provided.

  • You can select only ONE location from the drop-down menu.
  • It is not possible to exclude a particular location from your results.
  • Some material formats are also listed in the location menu: Journals, Compact Discs, DVDs, Dissertations/Theses, Microforms, and Electronic Resources.
  • When a location limit is used, items from that location will sort to the top of a multi-item list in the item summary.
  • Sometimes you will retrieve records using a location limit, but not see any items from that location in the item summary. This is because there is one or more withdrawn items from that location that is suppressed from view.

KNOWN ISSUE: Retention of Location Limits
When you pre-limit a search to a specific library location, that limit is retained when you select a different kind of search from the left-side search menu. For example, if you do a title search limited to DVDs, then decide to do an author search for a book, you must reset the limit to the ALL COLLECTIONS default; otherwise you'll do your author search only among DVDs. If you click the Start Over button after a search before selecting the new search type, the system will reset the default for you.

Material types and formats: You can select multiple entries from this menu by holding down the Control key (Windows) or Apple key (Macintosh) while clicking on desired entries.

Warning: Assignment of Material Types is not always consistent in the database. If you're not finding what you need, re-do your search without a material type limit.

Available Material Types include:
  • Books & Printed Materials (including Microforms)
  • Ebooks
  • DVDs
  • Videocassettes
  • Compact Discs (Music)

Searching Multiple Words (Keyword Search)

When searching multiple words the system will automatically supply the Boolean AND operator between each word. Words entered for the search will all occur somewhere in each retrieved record, though not necessarily near each other or in the order entered.

Other Boolean operators that may be applied to keyword searches are OR and AND NOT.

Examples:

egypt OR cairo – finds records where either word is present, but not necessarily both.

mercury AND NOT planet* -- finds records with the word "mercury" excluding those containing variations of the word "planet". Use the AND NOT operator with caution because you may inadvertently eliminate relevant results. It is most useful when trying to distinguish words that have distinctly different meanings or contexts.

Phrase Searches (Keyword Search)

Search for exact phrases by enclosing them in quotation marks "...". These words will appear together in all results exactly as typed. The phrase search overrides the relevancy ranking feature when processing results. Wildcards do not work in phrase searches.

Nested Searches (Keyword Search)

A nested keyword search query can be constructed using Boolean operators and parentheses (...) to establish the relationship among the terms. For example, to find records that contain the phrase "holy land" and either the word "palestine" or the word "israel," you would type:

"holy land" and (palestine or israel)

If your query contains more than one kind of Boolean operator, you must use parentheses to define the relationship of your terms, otherwise the search engine will not combine them as you intend. For example, the search
fish and farm or raising
should be typed as:
fish and (farm or raising)
If you leave out the parentheses, the search engine will rank the AND higher and give you results based on the logic
(fish and farm) or raising
which will not be useful.

Note: A nested search overrides the relevancy ranking feature when processing results.

Word Proximity Operator: ~N (Keyword Search)

You can type the operator ~N (tilde-N) following two keywords enclosed in quotes to retrieve records that contain the two words within N words of each other, in any order. For example you could enter this query into a keyword search box:

"fish farm"~2

to get records that have the terms "fish" and "farm" in close proximity to each other (up to two intervening words). This helps to increase the relevance of your search results. Wildcards do not work within quotes.

Truncation and Wildcards (Keyword Search)

* The asterisk matches any number of non-space characters, starting at the specified position in the word. For example, "intern*" will match "intern", "internal", "internet" and "international". The asterisk wildcard may also be embedded in a word. For example, "colo*r" would match both "color" and "colour" and "sul*ur" would match both "sulfur" and "sulphur".

? You may use a question mark to mask a single character anywhere within a word (e.g. wom?n will retrieve both "woman" and "women"). The ? wildcard symbol must be preceded by at least two characters. For example, "m?n" will return no results.

Making a truncation too generic will result in slow response times and too many results. Avoid queries such as un* or inter* or tex* that would retrieve a very large number of results. Wildcards do not work inside quotes.

Searching for Books in Series

Many scholarly books are published as part of ongoing series. Volumes in a series may or may not be numbered. Other series are classified separately according to each volume's topic and are thus scattered in the library. In both cases, the series title and numbering are included and searchable in their bibliographic records.

The most effective way to locate a specific book within an analyzed or separately classified series is to use the Advanced Search form to do a keyword search for distinctive words in the series title along with a volume number. For example, to find volume 1029 of the series Annals of the New York Academy of Sciences, type
annals new york 1029
in the search box. This should pull the correct record up immediately. Series that are cataloged this way will not be found using the Journal Title search option because they are not cataloged as serials.

For serials that are not analyzed -- primarily those that do not have any volume-specific titles or editors -- all volumes will be listed under a single record. Search for these items using the Title or Journal Title search option. Example: Annual Review of Earth and Planetary Sciences. The method described above for locating specific volumes in an analyzed series will not work with this kind of serial publication.

Subject Heading or Keyword?

Doing a Subject Heading search or using topical terms in a Keyword search are two different ways of doing the same thing -- finding materials on a particular topic.

Keyword searches are more general and will retrieve materials that have the desired terms not only in Subject Headings, but anywhere in the record. Your results will be more numerous but less focused, and there may be some results that are not relevant at all.

Subject Heading searches are more focused, and rely on human catalogers who have assigned specific controlled vocabulary descriptors to a particular work. Subject heading assignment is not always consistent over time or across subject areas. If you rely solely on subject headings you may miss relevant materials that do not have certain headings assigned to them. In addition, not all records in the Catalog have subject headings -- works of fiction, music and art, and other materials with minimal cataloging often lack subject headings.

It's a good idea to use both techniques when searching by topic. Start with a keyword search, and examine the subject headings found on relevant records, then search those as well to find other materials that may not contain the keywords you started with.

Punctuation is usually ignored by the search engine, with the following exceptions in Keyword searches:

SPECIAL AND NON-LATIN CHARACTERS

Diacritics and Special Characters

Special characters and diacritic marks should display when they are present in the catalog record. However, many records do not reliably contain these modified characters and display without them.

In most cases you can type or paste a search query with special characters and the system will search for only that modified character, retrieving matching words with the diacritic. Thus, a search for "étranger" will not retrieve "etranger". However, if you search "etranger" without the diacritic you will retrieve all records with or without the diacritic. For this reason it is better to leave the diacritics off your search terms to ensure full retrieval.

If your browser displays these characters incorrectly, using non-alpha characters such as boxes or dots, some fonts and encoding settings on your computer may be missing or set improperly. Contact library staff for assistance.

Keyword search results are automatically sorted using a relevancy algorithm. Records retrieved are divided into relevancy categories: Most Relevant, Highly Relevant, Very Relevant, Relevant, and Other Relevant.   • RECENTLY CHECKED IN – Item was checked in within the last day and may still be on its way back to the shelf. Check the shelf and ask for assistance if you can't find it.
  • IN TRANSIT – Item is on its way to another library. When the item is checked in at its destination, it will no longer show "in transit".
  • ON HOLD SHELF – Currently being held for a borrower who has requested it.
  • ASK AT DESK – Ask library staff for further information.
  • BEING SEARCHED – Not on the shelf and currently being searched for by staff.
  • MISSING – Has been searched by staff but was not located. You can request missing titles via Interlibrary Services.
  • OVERDUE BILLED – Checked out, overdue, and borrower has been billed for the item. The item may be returned eventually. You can request overdue billed titles via Interlibrary Services.
  • LOST BILLED – Item was lost and billed to a borrower in our legacy system before May 2007; it is no longer available. You can request lost billed titles via Interlibrary Services.
  • LOST PAID – Item was lost and paid for by a borrower; it is no longer available. You can request lost paid titles via Interlibrary Services.

    Note: Lost and missing items are not routinely replaced. If you wish to request that the library replace such an item, please submit a Purchase Request Form.

  • RECENTLY ADDED – Item was added to the catalog within the last week or so. It may not yet be on the shelf. Ask staff if you can't find it.
  • AT BINDERY – A periodical currently out for binding and unavailable. Check for electronic access or use Interlibrary Services to request articles from volumes at bindery, and make a note of the bindery status in the request.
  • BEING BOUND – Unavailable pending binding by library staff.
  • BEING REPAIRED – Being repaired in PCL Preservation Services. The item is not available.
  • IN UNIT REPAIR – Being repaired in the owning unit.
  • IN PROCESS – Being processed and not yet available. You can place a Request on it and it will be held for you when it is ready.
  • UNAVAILABLE – Not currently available for checkout; ask library staff for further information.
  • STAFF USE – Unavailable to borrowers. Ask staff for further information.

In addition to the item statuses above, materials that have been ordered but not yet received or processed are also included in the Catalog:
  • ORDERED for [Location] – This display, which appears only on the Full Record screen, indicates that the book has been ordered but has not yet arrived.
You can click the Request button to place a hold on these ordered materials, and after it arrives and is processed you will be notified that it is available on the hold shelf at the owning location. (If the item is designated non-circulating or otherwise restricted, your hold will be cancelled and you'll be notified of this.)

Placing a hold on an item that has not yet arrived does not expedite receipt of the book from the vendor or publisher. It is not possible to rush the shipping of an order that has already been placed, but it will be processed promptly upon arrival.

Latest Received display

You can view current issue receipt information for printed journals, periodicals, magazines and other serial-type publications that are currently received in the Libraries. If you have trouble locating a specific issue of a periodical, ask staff in the owning library unit for assistance.

Multiple volumes/copies display

Many bibliographic records have multiple pieces attached to them (such as journals, multi-volume sets, and multiple copies). On the Brief Results display, up to three items are shown, with a note that there are additional copies or volumes.

On the Full Record display, up to ten items are listed. If more than ten items are attached, click on the button at the bottom to "View additional copies or search for a specific volume/copy." This will display the entire list of items. Serials are listed in reverse chronological order. Non-serial materials, such as encyclopedia sets, are listed in volume-number order.

Note: The 3/10 display limit is determined by the software, and we can't change this locally.

To find a specific volume of a serial or periodical with more than ten attached pieces, it's best to display and scan the entire list. Optionally, you can enter a volume number or year in the box below the 10-item short display, and then click the View additional... button. (If the volume you want does not appear, clear the box and view the entire list. Variations in the way volume numbers and years are indicated in call numbers may cause a specific volume not to show up in a search.)

Web Links, Book Covers, and Tags

  • Find It @ UT button: This button, which appears on any catalog record that contains an ISBN or ISSN, will take you to a menu of available options specific to this item, which may or may not include full text access to the content. This button is the primary link from the Catalog to electronic journal content. For books, the menu may contain links to e-book providers, but this is not comprehensive.

  • "Electronic Resource" link: This link appears in a brief record whenever there is a URL present in a bibliographic record. Many different kinds of links may be found: a full text e-book, a government document on the Web, an electronic dissertation or thesis, etc. For periodicals held in the Harry Ransom Center, the link goes to HRC's local periodicals holdings database. View the full record to see more information about where this link will take you. To open this link in a new browser window or tab, right-click on the link.

  • Book Covers: The Catalog links via a book's ISBN to Amazon.com to provide additional information related to certain materials. Amazon's thumbnail cover image, when available, is displayed on the brief and full record pages. Click on the cover to go to the Amazon information page for the book, where you may use services such as reader reviews and tags, editorial reviews, and "Search Inside" features, and purchase a copy.

    Occasionally the cover image does not match the book in the catalog record. This is due to an ISBN conflict or error, which is usually the fault of the publisher or Amazon. If the ISBN in our record matches the physical item, there is nothing the Libraries can do to fix it.

  • Google Books: A button links to Google Books when Google offers preview or full-text content for that title. This link looks for an exact match for the bibliographic edition in the Catalog; other editions may be available if you search Google Books directly. This is especially true if you're looking for an e-book version of a classic out-of-copyright work that has been republished in multiple editions. See also under Viewing Electronic Books below.

  • Permanent Link: If you want to bookmark a specific record in your browser, or copy a persistent link to that record for use elsewhere, click on "Bookmark Link." This will reload the current record using a stable URL. Then do a bookmark/add to favorites command in your browser, or copy the URL from the browser address line.

  • LibraryThing: User tags from LibraryThing.com are displayed in a box on the full record screen, when this information is available for a book. The "Recommended Books" list shows other titles that have similar tags, as well as ISBN links to alternate editions and translations of the same work. Clicking on a tag will open a tag browser window which shows related tags and a list of books in the Library Catalog that share that tag. LibraryThing tags are contributed by users around the world. Tags are not maintained or controlled by UT Libraries, and we can't remove "bad" tags. To contribute tags of your own, you need to register for a free account directly on the LibraryThing site.

  • Text/SMS: You can text the call number of a specific item to your mobile phone. Service provider charges may apply.

Marking, Saving and Exporting Records

The Catalog allows you to mark and save bibliographic records. These records are temporarily saved to a "clipboard" area awaiting your action. From the clipboard you can either export/email records immediately, or save them indefinitely to your "My Lists" area in your account for later action. Records saved to the Clipboard are deleted after 20 minutes, or when you close your browser, whichever comes first. To avoid losing your work over a longer period of time, use the My Lists function instead.

  1. To mark a record, click the "Save to Clipboard" button in a brief or full record display. After you do this you will see buttons to "View Clipboard" and "Empty Clipboard" at the top of the screen. The "Add to Clipboard" button for a selected record will change to a "Remove From Clipboard" button. A "Save All to Clipboard" button is available on the Index display from browse search results. This saves only the records displayed on that screen, not the entirety of your search results.
  2. Click the "View Clipboard" button to see the list of your marked records. To delete selected records from the list, mark their checkboxes and click the Delete Selected button.
  3. To export/email, click the Export Saved button.
  4. Choose the format for export: Full Display (the default); or format them for EndNote/RefWorks bibliographic management software. [More information on EndNote use]

    KNOWN ISSUE: The call number(s) is exported only in the Full Display format. (Call numbers are not included in the EndNote format.) The call numbers of all attached items will be included in the full record export, so if you export a record for a periodical you will get a list of call numbers representing all the attached volumes for that title.

  5. Choose a destination for the records: E-mail (enter your address in the box provided); your browser screen (for printing); or download a file to a local disk.
  6. Click Submit. If you chose to e-mail, a confirmation message will display. If you chose to download to a file, you will be asked for file information. To return to your place in the catalog, click the Previous Screen button.
  7. If you choose to print the records from the screen export, your printout will include the accompanying graphics and layout of the catalog web pages. For a cleaner, text-only printout, you should first email or export the records, and then print from a file.
  8. To clear your saved records, click the "Empty Clipboard" button.

The My Lists feature allows you to save selected records to lists you create within your library account. Unlike the Clipboard, these saved records will remain in your account until you delete them.

  1. Add desired records to the Clipboard and then click View Clipboard.
  2. Edit your list and click Add to My Lists.
  3. Log in with your UT EID if you are not already logged in.
  4. Select an existing list or create a new one.
  5. To view your lists, click the My Record button.
  6. You can delete selected records or export the list.

    KNOWN ISSUE: The "Add Marked to My Lists" button works only from browse-type index screens, where entries have checkboxes that can be marked. It has no function from brief bibliographic display screens or in keyword search results.

Logging In

To log in to your library account space, click the "Renew & Library Account" button at the top of any screen. Enter your valid UT EID and password. You will be taken to your patron record display, which offers a number of options. To log out, just click the Logout button at the top of any screen. Be sure to log out of your account whenever you leave a shared or public workstation.

KNOWN ISSUE: If your EID password contains an angle-bracket character (< or > ), you will not be able to log into the Catalog system. This is due to a security feature coded into the system by the software vendor. You will have to change your password to replace those characters in order to log in.

Renewing Your Books

Library borrowers may renew most of their charged materials online. Exceptions to this include items with recalls or holds; short-period loan items (such as Reserves materials); items overdue and billed.

  1. Log in to your My Account space using your UT EID and password.
  2. You will see the items checked out to you.
  3. Select the materials you wish to renew by checking the boxes in the RENEW column and then click the Renew Selected button. If you want to renew everything, click the Renew ALL button.
  4. If you cannot renew items, a message will appear on the screen. If you are successful, a new due date will appear in the "STATUS" column. Always check the STATUS column for information on the success or failure of your renewal.
  5. If you aren't allowed to renew and the message "Highest level overdue" displays, this means that a book checked out to you has been recalled, and is now overdue. Your checkout and renewal privileges are blocked until the book is returned.
  6. You may not be able to renew a large number of items all at once with a single click of the Renew ALL button. If the system does not respond, try exiting My Account, going back in and renewing the items in smaller batches by checking boxes in the RENEW column and clicking the Renew Selected button.
  7. Review your list carefully to make sure everything has been renewed. If items cannot be or are not renewed in My Account, you are responsible for presenting them at a library circulation desk for renewal, or returning them to the library, by the date due, to avoid overdue charges.

Requesting Materials Retrieval from Campus Libraries

Most library materials that are currently available for check-out are eligible for retrieval and delivery to a campus library of your choice.

Materials Eligible for Retrieval and Delivery:

  • Regularly circulating books (those that circulate for 14 days or more)
  • Audio-visual items (those that circulate for 7 days or more)
  • Regularly circulating books in the Marine Science Library in Port Aransas

Materials NOT Eligible for Retrieval and Delivery:

  • Journal/Periodical volumes and issues
  • Materials on Reserves
  • Non-circulating and limited-circulation items
  • Maps
  • Selected microforms (depending on location)
  • Equipment
  • Materials held in the Ransom Center, Law Library, Briscoe Center for American History, and other non-circulating special collections

Note: We offer this retrieval service for the convenience of our patrons. There is a 3-5 day turnaround time for Austin items, and rush service is not available. The requested item will remain on the shelf and available to other users until it can be retrieved by UT Libraries staff. The fastest way to obtain any available UT Libraries item is to visit the library in person.

  1. To request retrieval and delivery of an eligible item, click the Request link on the record. In the brief display, this link is shown to the right; on the full record display it is found in the row of links at the top. The Request link will not be shown on records that are not eligible for this service.
  2. Log in with your UT EID. You must have a UT EID and current checkout privileges to use this service.
  3. A list of items will display. Requestable items will have a radio button. Note: Some materials in storage are restricted, and these are not requestable online. Submit a paper retrieval request in person at the circulation desk of the owning library.
  4. Select the desired item. (If you need multiple volumes of a title, you must request each volume separately. The system does not allow a single request for multiple items.) Click REQUEST SELECTED ITEM at the bottom.
  5. Choose a pickup location. You may choose the owning library if you wish.
  6. The system will place a retrieval request and the item will be delivered to the Hold Shelf in the library you selected. You will be notified by email when it is ready for pickup. The item will be held for you for 7 days.
  7. Log out when you are finished.

Saved Searches and Alerts

If you have a favorite author or subject that you often search in the Library Catalog, you can save the search for later use. This will prevent you from having to key in the search each time.

  1. Log in to your My Library Account space using your UT EID and password.
  2. Do a search for your favorite author, subject, title, etc. or create an advanced keyword search including limits to material type, language, publication year, etc..
  3. When your results are displayed, click the button marked "Save as preferred search". You can save up to 25 searches this way. (Note: A keyword search must have at least one hit before you can save it -- there is no way to save a keyword search that has no results. However, you can save a title, author, ISBN, or other browse-type search that has no exact match.)
  4. The next time that you log in to your Account, you can click the "Preferred Searches" button to see a list of your saved searches.
  5. Click on the search link associated with any of the preferred search terms in the list to quickly execute your search.
  6. You can be alerted of new materials matching your searches added to the library collection. Just check the "Mark for Email" box and be sure that your patron record information includes your current email address. Searches will be run weekly on Sunday nights, and results will be emailed to your designated address.
  7. To turn off the email alert, just go to your Preferred Searches list, uncheck the Mark for Email box, and click Update List. You can delete all your saved searches by clicking the Clear all Searches button.
